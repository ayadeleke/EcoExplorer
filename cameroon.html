The Republic of Cameroon

Cameroon, a vibrant jewel situated in the heart of West Africa, unfolds its diverse landscapes like a magnificent tapestry. From the verdant embrace of rainforests teeming with life to the sun-drenched savannas and the volcanic peaks scraping the sky, Cameroon offers a breathtaking spectacle for the senses. Its rich history whispers tales of ancient kingdoms, powerful empires like the Bamum and the Sao, and resilient communities who have carved their stories into the land. Having experienced colonial rule under various European powers, Cameroon gained independence in 1960, marking the dawn of a new era characterized by self-governance and aspirations for development.


Endowed with abundant natural wealth, Cameroon's economy revolves around diverse sectors, with cocoa cultivation, oil exploration, and burgeoning tourism emerging as key drivers of growth alongside its renowned export of timber and coffee. The country takes pride in its rich biodiversity, which serves as a cornerstone of its burgeoning ecotourism sector. The lush rainforests harbor a myriad of plant species, including towering ebony trees and the coveted African mahogany. Its wildlife is equally impressive, boasting majestic lions, elusive leopards, and vibrant birdlife. Yet, amid this natural splendor, Cameroon faces pressing challenges to its precious biodiversity. Habitat destruction, illegal poaching, and the impacts of climate change threaten iconic species such as the African forest elephant and the critically endangered Cross River gorilla. In response, Cameroon has taken proactive measures, establishing protected areas and implementing stringent conservation policies to safeguard its natural heritage for future generations.


Culturally, Cameroon is a vibrant mosaic of traditions, languages, and customs, with over 200 ethnic groups contributing to its rich tapestry of heritage. From the bustling markets of Douala to the tranquil villages of the Grassfields, Cameroon pulsates with the rhythms of diversity, exemplifying the harmonious coexistence of its people. Festivals play a central role in Cameroonian culture, serving as occasions for communal revelry and spiritual expression. The Ngondo festival of the Sawa people, for instance, celebrates the ancestral spirits with colorful processions, traditional dances, and feasting on local delicacies. Music and dance are integral to Cameroonian identity, with genres like Makossa and Bikutsi captivating audiences both at home and abroad.


Despite its linguistic diversity, with French and English as official languages alongside numerous indigenous tongues, Cameroon remains united in its pursuit of progress. Governed as a democratic republic, the country faces challenges in areas such as infrastructure development and political stability. Yet, under the leadership of President Paul Biya, Cameroon is committed to charting a path towards sustainable development and prosperity for all its citizens. With its breathtaking landscapes, rich cultural heritage, and dynamic populace, Cameroon stands poised to emerge as a beacon of resilience and progress in the heart of Africa.

The Plant Species

Broad-leaved croton

Croton macrostachyus is a deciduous tree 3-25 m high, although more commonly 6-12 m; crown rounded and open with large spreading branches. Bark pale gray or gray-brown, finely reticulate, fairly smooth, finely fissured with age; slash reddish; shoots densely and shortly hairy.

Tiama

The tree known as Gedur-nohor in Nigeria, Tiama in Ivory Coast, can grow up to 50 meters in height. Its bole is moderately straight and cylindrical, ranging from 20 to 26 meters in length, with a diameter reaching 130 to 230 centimeters, often supported by large buttresses and wide-spreading root ridges.

Schefflera

The Umbrella Plant, scientifically known as Schefflera, is a vibrant and effortlessly cared for addition to indoor gardens. Its luxuriant green foliage and elegant demeanor have the power to instantaneously elevate the atmosphere of any living space, infusing it with a refreshing and inviting ambiance.

Guibourtia

The tropical tree Bubinga, found in the Congo Basin, reaches impressive heights of up to 60 meters with a diameter of 2 meters. Its distinguishing feature is its large, sinuous buttresses that develop from a rough, brown bole, which ages into reddish depressions. Locally, Bubinga is extensively utilized in traditional medicine for its bark and leaves.

Cordia platythyrsa

Cordia platythyrsa, also known as West African cordia, is a tall flowering tree belonging to the borage family (Boraginaceae) and is native to western and central Africa. Renowned for its soft wood, it is commonly utilized in furniture making and carpentry. However, due to extensive logging activities, it is classified as "vulnerable," highlighting the threat to its existence.

Bush candle

Senna alata, also known as emperor's candlesticks, candle bush, candelabra bush, Christmas candles, empress candle plant, ringworm shrub, or candletree, is a significant medicinal tree and ornamental flowering plant in the subfamily Caesalpinioideae. This remarkable species of Senna was occasionally classified under its own genus, Herpetica. The shrub typically reaches heights of 3–4 meters (9.8–13.1 feet) with leaves measuring 50–80 centimeters (20–31 inches) in length.

African cherry

Prunus africana, or African cherry, is widely distributed across Africa, thriving in montane regions of central and southern Africa and on islands like Bioko, São-Tomé, Grande Comore, and Madagascar. It grows to impressive heights of 30 to 40 meters, making it the tallest Prunus species. Known for its expansive crowns, it prefers moist climates with annual rainfall ranging from 900 to 3,400 millimeters (35 to 130 inches) and displays moderate frost tolerance.

Parasol tree

Parasol trees, increasingly popular for garden architecture, offer year-round interest and shade, often replacing pergolas and arbors. They provide privacy and improve with age unlike timber structures. Deciduous varieties, preferred for their seasonal adaptability, allow light in winter and shade in summer. Typically arranged in square or rectangular patterns, these trees have stem heights starting at around 230 centimeters, ensuring ample head clearance beneath their canopies.

False assegai

False assegai

Maesa lanceolata is a variable species, ranging from a straggling shrub to a small tree or a rounded bushy tree. Its lance-shaped leaves, measuring 40-180 x 25-130 mm, have toothed margins and are spirally arranged. Often mistaken for Curtisia dentata, the leaves of Maesa lanceolata can be distinguished by their alternate arrangement, whereas C. dentata has opposite leaves.

African pterygota

Pterygota bequaertii is a deciduous tree, reaching heights of up to 50 meters, with a branchless bole up to 2 meters high. It is classified as Vulnerable (VU) due to over-exploitation. The fruit typically comprises 1-5 ellipsoid-globular follicles, woody in nature, with stipes ranging from 1-5 cm in length, each containing numerous seeds. Seeds measure 5-8 cm long, including a wing spanning 1.5 cm to 4 cm. Its leaves are smaller compared to those of the relatively more common Pterygota macrocarpa.

The Animal Species

Bongo

The bongo (Tragelaphus eurycerus) is the largest and most colorful of the African forest antelopes, belonging to the spiral-horned antelope tribe Tragelaphini (family Bovidae). It ranks as the third heaviest antelope, following the giant eland and common eland.

Hippopotamus

Hippopotamus, the third-largest land mammal, are adapted for both water and land mobility. Their semi-aquatic lifestyle is supported by four-webbed toes for even weight distribution and powerful short legs for propulsion in water. Despite their size, they can move swiftly in both environments.

Giant Pangolin

The giant pangolin, the largest living pangolin species, can weigh up to 35 kg. It differs from smaller tree-dwelling African pangolins with its regular scale pattern, fossorial lifestyle, and lack of tail pads for climbing. While its range overlaps slightly with ground pangolins, it prefers moist habitats near water sources.

Gorilla

Gorillas are primarily herbivorous and are predominantly ground-dwelling great apes found in the tropical forests of equatorial Africa. The genus Gorilla consists of two species: the eastern gorilla and the western gorilla, with four or five subspecies identified. Gorillas share a high degree of DNA similarity with humans, ranging from 95 to 99%, making them the closest living relatives to humans after chimpanzees and bonobos.

African Elephant

African elephants are the largest terrestrial mammals on Earth. They are classified into two subspecies: the African bush elephant and the African forest elephant. Bush elephants are generally larger, with curved tusks, while forest elephants are darker in color and have straighter tusks that point downward. Both subspecies play crucial roles in their respective ecosystems and are vital to the health of African habitats.

Lion

The lion is the second-largest living big cat, following the tiger. Male lions are distinctive for their thick mane of brown or black hair, which surrounds their head and neck. This mane typically darkens with age, and a thicker, darker mane is often associated with better health in the cat. Unlike most other cat species, both male and female lions are capable of roaring, a vocalization that can be heard from distances of up to 8 kilometers away.

Leopard

Leopards are characterized by their distinctive coat patterns, which vary among the nine recognized subspecies. In warm, dry habitats, their coats tend to be tawny or light yellow, while in dense forests, they may appear reddish-orange. Their coats are adorned with dark, irregular spots known as rosettes. Interestingly, the shape of these rosettes differs between subspecies: they are circular in East African leopards and square in Southern African leopards.

Chimpanzee

Chimpanzees (Pan troglodytes) are primates that reside in the tropical forests of central Africa. They occupy a range spanning from about 10 degrees N to 8 degrees S latitude, and from 15 degrees W to 32 degrees E longitude. Their habitat extends from Gambia in the west to Uganda in the east, with the exception of the area bordered by the Congo and Lualaba rivers in central Zaire (Congo), where their sister species, bonobos (Pan paniscus), are prevalent.

African Wild Dog

The African wild dog, scientifically known as Lycaon pictus, is a wild canine indigenous to sub-Saharan Africa. Renowned for its striking coat, it is also referred to as the painted dog or Cape hunting dog. This canine species holds the distinction of being the largest of its kind on the African continent. Furthermore, it stands as the sole surviving member of the genus Lycaon, its specialized dentition is adapted for hypercarnivorous diets and the absence of dewclaws.

Forest Buffalo

The forest buffalo (Syncerus caffer nanus) is the smallest subspecies of the African buffalo. Unlike its larger relatives such as the Cape buffalo, West African savanna buffalo, and Central African savanna buffalo, it weighs only 250 to 320 kg (550-705 lbs) compared to the Cape buffalos' 400 to 800 kg (880-1760 lbs). Its reddish-brown hide, which darkens in the facial area, and the shape and size of its horns set it apart from other subspecies.
